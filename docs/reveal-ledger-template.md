# Reveal Ledger Template

Use this when continuity risk is less about where people are, and more about who knows what, when.

This is especially useful for:
- mysteries
- campaigns being adapted into prose
- fanfiction with canon knowledge constraints
- ensemble stories with secrets, false beliefs, or delayed reveals
- any project where recap notes blur player knowledge, character knowledge, and reader knowledge

## Purpose

A reveal ledger prevents knowledge-state continuity drift.

It forces the audit to separate:
- what is objectively true in the story world
- what each character believes
- what the reader is allowed to know at each point
- when a later reinterpretation becomes possible

## How to use it

Create one row for each important secret, disputed fact, hidden motive, mistaken belief, or reveal-sensitive event.

Update it whenever:
- a character learns something new
- a false belief is reinforced or broken
- the narration implies more than the viewpoint should know
- recap notes compress hindsight into earlier scenes

## Minimal version, if the project is messy

If the material is too rough for a full ledger, track just five things:
- secret or disputed fact
- who currently knows
- who only suspects
- when the reader should know
- what scene or session would break if this slips

## Common failure modes this catches

### 1. Narration knows too much too early

The prose explains motives before the viewpoint should be able to infer them.

### 2. Characters react to information they do not have yet

A decision feels smart on paper, but only because the draft accidentally gave the character access to future knowledge.

### 3. Recap notes harden rumour into fact

A campaign summary may flatten uncertainty that the prose version actually needs to preserve.

### 4. Reveals land twice, or not at all

A secret may be implicitly obvious long before the intended reveal, or so hidden that the later payoff feels unearned.

## Audit prompts to pair with the ledger

Ask these while filling it out:
- What is true here, versus what is merely believed?
- What does each major character know at this exact point?
- What is the reader meant to believe right now?
- Is the draft using hindsight language too early?
- If I remove out-of-world table knowledge, does the scene still make sense?
